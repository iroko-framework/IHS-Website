Bread Before the End: Havana and the Memory of Constantinople

On cities that keep going anyway
Iroko Commentary · History & Memory · March 2026
By Délé Fágbèmí Ọ̀.

What the final weeks of Constantinople tell us about cities that maintain their rhythms when the horizon is already full of smoke, and what Havana is doing right now. In the spring of 1453, the markets of Constantinople still opened in the morning.

In the spring of 1453, the markets of Constantinople still opened in the morning.

Bakers kneaded dough in narrow stone shops. Fishermen carried baskets of the night's catch through streets worn smooth by a thousand years of footsteps. Children ran between the columns of old forums and along alleys that had outlived emperors. The city still possessed its rhythms. Bread was baked. Bells rang for prayer. Merchants argued over prices.

Beyond the walls, the army of Sultan Mehmed II waited.

The Ottoman campfires stretched across the fields outside the great Theodosian Walls, a ring of light surrounding the ancient capital. Cannon fire rolled across the city day and night. Each blast shook the churches and monasteries that had stood since the age of Constantine. Dust drifted from vaulted ceilings. People paused in the middle of their work, listened for a moment, and then returned to it.

Inside the city, life continued.

The emperor, Constantine XI Palaiologos, walked the walls with his soldiers. Priests held liturgies that stretched long into the night. Citizens hauled stones, repaired breaches, and carried water to the defenders. At the same time the baker still opened his shop in the morning. A fisherman still sold what he had caught. A mother still scolded a child for wandering too far into the street.

This was not denial. Most people in Constantinople understood the balance of forces outside the walls. The empire that had ruled the eastern Mediterranean for a thousand years had already been reduced to a single city and a few scattered territories. The siege did not arrive as a surprise. It arrived as the final chapter of a long and visible decline.

What remained was the strange discipline of ordinary life.

Cities that sense their own fragility often become very good at this. Routine becomes a form of resistance. The act of opening a shop, baking bread, repairing a roof, or attending prayer becomes a quiet statement that the city still exists. Even when the horizon is filled with campfires, people insist on the small structures of daily life.

Accounts from the final weeks describe a population suspended between effort and acceptance. People worked constantly to reinforce the defenses, yet they also gathered in churches to pray through the night. Rumors moved through the streets as quickly as the wind off the Bosporus. Some spoke of miracles. Others spoke quietly of the end of an age.

Yet the markets still opened in the morning.

There is a particular atmosphere that settles over cities in moments like this. It is not panic, and it is not calm. It is something in between. The future is visible but has not yet arrived. People sense that the world they know may be nearing its end, but the present still demands attention. Someone must still bake bread. Someone must still mend a wall. Someone must still sweep the dust from the floor of a shop.

I have never lived inside a city under siege.

But as a student of history, I have long wondered what it must feel like to live inside one. Not during the final moment of catastrophe, but in the weeks and months before it arrives. The long interval when people continue their lives while something immense waits just beyond the horizon.

Lately, I find myself thinking about this because of Havana.

I am not there. I speak with people I love who live there. When we talk, they tell me that everything is fine. Life goes on. Someone found eggs that morning. A neighbor repaired a refrigerator with parts salvaged from another machine. Music drifts out of an open window somewhere down the street. These are ordinary conversations. They are the kinds of conversations people have every day.

And yet history has trained my imagination in a particular way.

When I listen, I sometimes picture another city long ago. I imagine the streets of Constantinople in the spring of 1453. I imagine people sending word to relatives in distant places that everything is normal, that the markets are still open, that bread is still being baked in the mornings.

Perhaps the people inside the city felt nothing unusual at all. Human beings are remarkably skilled at living inside uncertainty. The rhythms of daily life can continue even when the ground beneath them has begun to shift. But from a distance, the atmosphere looks different. From a distance, the lights of the campfires beyond the walls are easier to see.

The distance between a city and the world beyond it can change the way events are understood.

Inside Constantinople, the siege was immediate and physical. Cannons fired. Stones shattered. The defenders could see the tents of the Ottoman army stretching across the fields beyond the walls. Yet even then, the city did not experience every hour as a crisis. Life unfolded in fragments of ordinary time. Someone walked to market. Someone carried water from a cistern. Someone repaired a door that had come loose from its hinges.

From far away, the story compresses. Historians speak of the siege as if it were a single dramatic moment. In reality, it lasted weeks. The people inside the city woke each morning and faced the same strange mixture of routine and uncertainty. The walls still stood. The churches still held services. The markets still functioned, though supplies grew thinner.

Human beings are remarkably capable of living within that kind of suspended time.

The mind adjusts. The extraordinary becomes familiar. Even the sound of cannon fire can become part of the background of daily life. A person pauses for a moment, listens, and then returns to whatever task is at hand. The baker must still prepare the next batch of bread. The fisherman must still clean his nets.

There is a quiet dignity in this persistence.

Cities are not only made of stone walls and towers. They are made of habits. They are made of routines that repeat themselves day after day until they feel permanent. When those routines continue under pressure, they create the appearance that the city itself remains unchanged. The market stall still opens in the morning. The door of the church still swings inward. The same voices greet each other in the same streets.

From inside the city, this continuity can feel reassuring. From outside, it can look fragile.

When I speak with friends and family in Havana, our conversations often revolve around small things. A repair completed. A long line that moved faster than expected. A neighbor who managed to obtain something difficult to find. These are practical details of everyday life. They are the language of survival in any large city.

What strikes me is how familiar this pattern feels when viewed through the lens of history.

People who live inside a place develop a natural loyalty to the present moment. They build their lives around the structures that already exist. Even when those structures show signs of strain, daily life tends to continue along the same paths it followed before. The market still opens. The bus still arrives, even if it arrives late. Someone still sits on a balcony in the evening while music drifts up from the street.

None of this is unusual. It is simply how people live.

Yet historians learn to notice something else that often appears at the edges of such moments. Beneath the visible routines, there can exist a quiet awareness that circumstances are changing in ways that no one fully understands yet. The signs are subtle. Conversations linger on certain topics. Rumors travel more quickly than before. People begin to store away objects or knowledge they might need later.

In Constantinople, this awareness appeared in small gestures.

Chroniclers describe churches filled with people praying through the night. Scholars continued copying manuscripts and preserving texts that had survived from earlier centuries. Citizens worked to reinforce the defenses even as daily life continued around them. These actions suggest a population that understood something important: the city might endure. It might also change forever. No one could say which future would arrive.

In such moments, people rarely abandon their routines. Instead, they hold onto them more tightly. Ordinary life becomes a kind of anchor. The familiar gestures of each day reassure the mind that the world has not yet broken apart.

There is another thing that happens in cities facing uncertainty. People begin to think about what must survive them.

In Constantinople, as the siege tightened, Byzantine scholars made decisions about which manuscripts to pack and which to leave behind. They carried codices westward into exile, tucking centuries of philosophy and theology and history into the holds of Venetian ships. When the city finally fell, some of that knowledge found its way to Italian courts and monastic libraries, where it entered a new circulation. Historians have long argued about how much of what we call the Renaissance owed something to those fragile migrations of text.

The people doing the packing did not know they were seeding a renaissance. They were simply doing what people in endangered cities have always done: deciding, quietly and urgently, what the world must not be allowed to forget.

In Havana, something similar is happening, though the medium and the method are different.

I think about this often. The city holds an enormous reservoir of living knowledge, the kind that does not travel well and does not file neatly into archives. Songs learned within families. Ritual practices transmitted hand to hand across generations. Ways of understanding the world that were preserved through centuries of pressure precisely because they were not written down, not indexed, not accessible to those who would have destroyed them. Some of the deepest archives in Havana exist entirely in living memory.

What happens to that knowledge when a city moves toward rupture? Sometimes it scatters. Sometimes it concentrates. Sometimes people begin quietly gathering what they know and passing it outward with more urgency than before.

I do not know exactly what is being preserved right now in the neighborhoods of Havana. But I know that the impulse to preserve is ancient and persistent. It does not require crisis to awaken, but crisis sharpens it.

There is a legend about the fall of Constantinople that has stayed with me for years.

When the Ottoman soldiers finally breached the walls on the morning of May 29, 1453, a priest was said to be celebrating liturgy inside the great church of Hagia Sophia. As the soldiers entered, the priest walked toward the wall of the apse and disappeared into the stone. He is waiting there still, the legend says. He will emerge to finish the liturgy when the city is restored.

It is a story about interrupted ceremony. It is a story about the refusal to accept that something sacred can be permanently extinguished. The liturgy did not end. It only paused, drawn into the walls of the city itself, held in suspension until the conditions for its completion return.

Every city that faces collapse invents some version of this story. Something was hidden. Something went underground. Something is waiting.

I do not think the story is literally true. But I think it expresses something that is true about the relationship between people and the places they love. Cities carry knowledge in their walls. When cities change, that knowledge does not simply vanish. It disperses. It hides. It travels in the memories of people who leave, and it endures quietly in the habits of those who stay.

The markets of Constantinople opened in the morning until the morning they did not.

Somewhere in Havana today, someone is standing in a line, tracking down something difficult to find, repairing something with parts taken from something else. Music is coming from an open window. A child is being scolded for wandering too far. The city is doing what cities do: insisting on the present, holding the future at arm's length, performing the ordinary with stubborn and remarkable grace.

I watch from a distance. I listen to what my loved ones tell me. History has given me a particular kind of sight that is not always a comfort. I see the campfires beyond the walls, even when the people inside the city have grown used to them.

But I also see the priest walking into the wall.

I see the scholars rolling manuscripts into leather cases in the middle of the night. I see the baker opening his shop in the morning.

Someone always sells bread before the end of the world.

And someone always carries something precious out of the city when the walls finally come down.
