Our Vision

The Iroko Historical Society brings together practitioners and scholars of African-rooted traditions to build a permanent institutional home for knowledge that has circulated, for centuries, outside formal archives.

Our work centers on responsible acquisition, culturally informed description, and staged access to sacred and community materials. We are preparing these collections for the long term: the 22nd century and beyond.

We are actively building capacity to accept donations of archival materials, including personal libraries, manuscript notes, photographs, and digital media. If you hold materials relevant to Africana religious practice and cultural heritage, we invite you to contact us to discuss potential contributions.

The Iroko Tree

Iroko is one of many names for the "God Tree." This tree is an abode or meeting place for many classes of spirits: terrestrial and celestial, benevolent and malevolent, spirits immemorial and Johnny-come-latelies. At least four species of trees have been identified with the name Iroko, some native to Africa and others to tropical America, all now found across the globe. Some other names include Aragba, La Ceiba, Loko, Nsan, Mama Equndi, Kambala, Odum, Oji, and Igi-Olorun.

Promises made to Iroko should not be made lightly and never broken. All are welcome under the ever-expanding hallowed canopy of Iroko's bosomy embrace.

Access and Support

The Society offers access to its collections for qualified scholars and practitioners. Some material is available with open access online and in-house, while other holdings are subject to restrictions that respect donor wishes and the initiatory nature of the knowledge in question. See our Access and Use Policy for details.

We welcome all levels of support, from material donations and operational funding to partnership inquiries. For current giving opportunities, contact us at senior.curator@irokosociety.org.
