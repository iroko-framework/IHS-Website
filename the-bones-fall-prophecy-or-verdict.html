The Bones Fall:

Prophecy or Verdict?
Iroko Commentary · Sacred Speech & Practice · May 2026
By Délé Fágbèmí Ọ̀.

On Divinatory Inflation and the Ethics of Sacred Speech

On divination, divinatory inflation, and the ethics of sacred speech. What happens when a warning becomes a destiny, a proverb becomes a verdict, and a person must live inside a message that was never meant to be their final address.

The bones fall, but they do not speak alone. Someone gathers them, reads them, translates them, gives them scale, and sends their meaning out into the world, into the life of another person. Between the fall of the bones and the life that must now carry their message, there is a human chain of interpretation. It is there, in that chain, that prophecy can become verdict.

I have been thinking about this through my own itás, not only in terms of what they said, but in terms of how they arrived, how much authority they carried, how large they sounded, and what happened inside me as I tried to receive them. I remember the grandeur of the pronouncements, the feeling that something immense had been spoken over me, the sense that my life had suddenly been placed under a sky too vast for ordinary breathing. I remember being told things that sounded cosmic, ancestral, fated, morally charged, and spiritually consequential, while I remained a person sitting there trying to understand the words as they moved from another language into mine, from sound into thought, and from thought into the private theater where fear, faith, memory, and obedience all begin negotiating with each other.

And I remember shrinking.

That is the word that keeps returning to me. The revelations did not enlarge me, or at least not in the way I had been prepared to imagine religious revelation might. They made me feel smaller at the foot of the story being told about me. The pronouncements were vast, but I was still ordinary. They seemed to describe a person burdened by destiny, watched by powers, surrounded by danger, and implicated in a scale of meaning that I did not know how to inhabit. Instead of recognizing myself, I often found myself doubting whether I knew myself at all.

This is where my thinking begins, not with a rejection of divination, not with a denunciation of the traditions through which I came to know divination, but with a problem I call divinatory inflation.

By divinatory inflation, I mean the process by which religious consultation expands beyond proportion, so that a warning becomes a destiny, a proverb becomes a verdict, a tendency becomes an identity, and a possible danger becomes the governing explanation for a person’s life. It is not simply exaggeration, and it is not necessarily fraud. It is the inflation of religious speech until the person who receives it has less room to move, less room to interpret, less room to test, and less room to remain human in the face of what has been spoken. My concern comes from proximity, not contempt. It comes from having been shaped by religious consultation, having been wounded by it, having witnessed its effects on others, and having also been the diviner, not always as careful as I now understand I should have been.

The Problem of Scale

A consultation can contain truth and still be disproportionate. It can be rooted in a legitimate system and still overwhelm the person receiving it. It can intend guidance and still produce fear, dependence, or confusion. It can name something real while handing that reality to the recipient in a form that makes the person smaller than the message. That is the problem of scale.

What I heard in my itás often sounded too grand to be me. That sentence is not meant as false humility. I am saying that the person described by the pronouncements did not always feel recognizable. He sounded mythic, fated, burdened, watched, endangered. He sounded like someone living inside a religious drama that had already interpreted him before he could speak back. The gap between the self one knows and the self described by the consultation is what I mean by the crisis of recognition. Because the speech carries sacred authority, the lack of recognition may not feel like evidence that the consultation has been overstated. It may feel like evidence that the person does not yet understand themselves. Then doubt becomes obedience. This is how interpretive systems become sealed.

They absorb resistance as confirmation.

A good consultation should return a person to life with more capacity, not less. It should clarify without consuming, warn without imprisoning, reveal without replacing the person’s own conscience, memory, and experience. The consultations that troubled me most did not do that. I did not always feel seen by the revelation. Sometimes I felt overwritten by it.

A Fragile Translation Carrying Total Authority

One reason the grandness troubled me is that the revelation did not arrive whole. It passed through translation. In the context of itá, everyone knows that speech moves through people, yet the authority of the moment can make that human chain feel less important than it is. By the time the message reaches the person receiving counsel, it can feel as if the sacred itself has spoken in a single, uninterrupted voice, even though the event has been mediated from the beginning.

In my own case, the passage was long. The odú became proverb. The proverb became selection. Selection became the italero’s emphasis. Emphasis became interpretation. Interpretation moved through Spanish. Spanish moved into English. English moved into my ears. My ears carried it into my head. My head carried it into memory, anxiety, longing, imagination, and eventually into my hands, where I had to decide how to write it down, how to understand it, how to obey it, how to resist it, or how to live beneath it.

That is a long road for revelation to travel. Each stage promised clarification, but each stage also introduced vulnerability: tone, emphasis, fatigue, hierarchy, fear, expectation, the personality of the speaker, the emotional state of the listener, the instability of translation, and the pressure of a ritual moment in which everyone expects meaning to arrive with weight. Meaning may sharpen as it travels, but it may also blur, harden, inflate, or become detached from its original proportion. By the time the itá reached me, I could not always tell where revelation ended and interpretation began.

That uncertainty mattered because the authority of the itá did not feel uncertain. The words might require translation, but the obligation did not. I was being asked to receive the consultation as truth, warning, map, discipline, and destiny while I was still trying to understand what had actually been said. That is one of the most dangerous arrangements in religious life: authority arriving before intelligibility. A fragile translation was asked to carry total authority.

The diviner may speak once, but the recipient may hear those words for years. A phrase spoken in a room becomes a private weather system. A warning becomes a lens through which every failure is reread. The original context fades, but the force remains. This is why what happens after the consultation matters as much as the consultation itself.

On Both Sides of the Pronouncement

I have also been on the other side. This is the part that keeps the essay from becoming easy. I know what it is to receive a pronouncement too large to inhabit, but I have also been in the room when something was said about another person and watched the room begin to organize itself around that speech. A warning becomes a reputation faster than anyone intends. A possibility hardens into private certainty before it has been examined. Collective attention mistakes itself for discernment.

Prophecy has a double life: it acts on the person named, but it also acts through the people who hear it. Everyone who hears a pronouncement becomes a translator, deciding consciously or not how much force to give it, whether to hold it as warning, possibility, or verdict, whether to leave the person space to remain human or to begin reading every gesture through the prophecy.

I have been part of that listening world. It would be dishonest to write as if the harm only moved toward me and never through me. There have been times when I accepted the interpretive frame too quickly, when I allowed a pronouncement to influence how I saw someone, when I treated the aura around a message as if it were proof of its proper application. Communities become agents of divinatory inflation in exactly this way. The message may begin as something conditional, but repetition, fear, and communal memory can remove the condition. By the time the person is being treated according to the pronouncement, it may no longer be remembered as something spoken in a particular context by a particular person through a particular chain of interpretation. It becomes simply what is known. And that phrase should frighten us.

Once a person is “known” in this way, evidence begins to gather around the name, not because evidence is neutral, but because perception has been trained. The prophecy does not merely reveal. It teaches people how to look, and once people have been taught how to look, they begin finding what they have been trained to see.

But the harm does not only move outward through community. It can move inward, into the person’s own cognition. Think of the person who has been told that his judgment is spiritually compromised, that his mind operates under a condition that distorts perception and choice. The verdict is not that he did something wrong. It is that his brain is dislocated, that the organ of his decision-making cannot be trusted. Once that verdict lands, something strange and corrosive begins. He no longer makes a bad decision and reckons with it. He makes a decision, watches it go wrong, and reads the outcome as confirmation of the original pronouncement. The verdict has gotten inside the process. Every failure proves the sentence. Every error is no longer evidence of ordinary human fallibility but of the specific spiritual condition that was named. He is no longer learning from mistakes. He is fulfilling a prophecy.

Having Been the Diviner

I have also been the diviner, and I cannot leave that out without making this essay dishonest. I have sat with signs, cards, bones, patterns, spirits, and felt the pressure to say something meaningful. There is a charged silence in those encounters: the person across from you wants not merely information but orientation, and the diviner feels that want as a kind of gravity. The desire to help is real. So is the temptation, not always resisted, to make the message strong enough that it will be heard.

Sincerity is not enough. A diviner can be sincere and still be careless with scale. The diviner’s responsibility does not end with accuracy. Proportion matters, and so does mercy, clarity, and grace. A person may carry one sentence for a lifetime, which means the more sacred the speech appears to be, the more human care it requires. The speaker does not control the afterlife of the speech. What the diviner sends as a warning, the social world may receive as a sentence.

This is why I now think divination is not only the art of seeing. It is the discipline of speaking what is seen without making the person smaller than the vision. It means resisting the theater of certainty, refusing the pleasure of being impressive, knowing when a message should be held as a question rather than delivered as a conclusion, and having the humility to say, this is how I understand it, rather than, this is the final meaning of your life. I am writing toward an ethics I did not always possess.

What I Am Left With

I am left with the image of myself trying to live under pronouncements too large for me, carrying sacred language from my ears into my head and eventually into my hands, where it became writing, action, hesitation, fear, and interpretation. I am left with the question of whether I was being revealed or overwritten, and with the strange grief of having been told something important about my life and not knowing whether the importance belonged to the sacred source, the tradition, the translator, the moment, my fear, or some mixture of them all.

I am also left with the image of the person marked by a negative pronouncement, whose possible betrayal became, in the social imagination, an already existing truth. That image stays with me because it shows how prophecy can leave the interior life and become communal discipline, how a warning can gather witnesses, how suspicion can create evidence, how a person can be sentenced into an interpretation before they have acted.

Religious consultation can speak truth. The question is whether truth is being carried with proportion, humility, and care for the person who must live inside it after the diviner has left the room. Beneath all of it, the question that stays: could a revelation have been true in some way and still handed to me in a form that made me shrink? I think the answer is yes.

A warning should not become a reputation. A community should not confuse suspicion with discernment. A diviner should not mistake intensity for truth. And a fragile translation should never be forced to carry total authority over a life.

And no one should have to shrink in order for revelation to seem large.
