**State of the Society**
*July 14, 2026*

Iroko Historical Society
Foundation Day Address
State of the Society

Year One asked what the Society intended to become. Year Two asked whether that intention could be built, tested, and made to hold weight under actual use. This address is the record of that test.

Year Two moved the Society from declared intention toward working infrastructure. Per Medjat, the Society's governed collections interface, launched this year at medjat.irokosociety.org. It holds two components under a single access architecture. The Medjat Library, a public research catalog, grew from a small pilot into a collection of more than six hundred cataloged records, spanning theses, journal articles, books, and book chapters on Afro-Atlantic sacred knowledge systems. The Medjat Archives holds unpublished and field-collected material under the same six-tier system, calibrated so that a single record can be public at one property and restricted to initiated practitioners at another. The Ewé Sacred Plant Database launched as a fifty-record technical pilot. Its purpose was not to publish a finished research resource but to prove that the Framework's field-level access control and the public interface actually worked, using structured test material drawn from Pierre Fatumbi Verger's fieldwork. A fuller corpus of more than seven hundred fifty records, built on operational data, remains in development. The Iroko Framework, the vocabulary that governs description and access across all of this, is deposited under a persistent Zenodo DOI (10.5281/zenodo.19157679) at version 1.3.0, with sixteen modules complete: one foundation module, five governance modules, and ten tradition-specific domain modules, alongside a supplementary alignment reference for interoperability with existing metadata standards. A further version, reflecting a year of continued development, is being prepared for deposit before Year Two closes.

None of this was built by hand alone. Five internal tools carried the year's work. Medjat Acquire searches external catalogs and imports records with acquisition provenance attached. Medjat Steward proposes description and access tags for human review rather than applying them automatically, and quarantines rather than deletes when a record's status is uncertain. Medjat Enrich backfills authority records, abstracts, and bibliographic detail onto holdings already in the catalog. A featured-work rotation keeps the public catalog's front page current. And the Iroko Framework Manager is the tool that took the vocabulary from two documented modules to sixteen. These tools are not incidental to the mission; they are the mechanism by which the Society's stated commitments to stewardship become structural rather than aspirational.

That mechanism matters because the Society does not treat digitization as disclosure. A catalog can grow by hundreds of records in a year without any of what those records govern becoming public by default. The Medjat Library's growth this year was overwhelmingly at the public tier, as published scholarship should be; the Medjat Archives is not measured the same way, and will not be. The Society recognizes more than one form of authority over knowledge. Institutional description is not the same thing as community consent, and a record's presence in a catalog is not evidence that everything about it may be known. Community-held and restricted material is not monetized, and it is not made public simply because the technical infrastructure to do so now exists. Where tagging and enrichment have not yet reached a record, that is not neglect. It is the ordinary pace of a review process that requires a person, not a script, to make the call. This pace is not a limitation the Society works against. It is a standard the Society has set for itself: growth measured against what can be governed responsibly, not against what infrastructure alone would allow. A tool capable of ingesting a thousand records in a week is not a reason to release a thousand records in a week.

The founder's own research continued this year, and it is worth naming precisely what belongs to whom. "Havana to the Sabine," the founder's study of documentary practice in the post-1808 Gulf slave trade, was submitted to *The American Archivist* (vol. 90.1) for peer review in June, and was named the University of Alabama Outstanding Student Paper for 2026. "Sealed from the Inside," a study of archival latency in Abakuá, Bizango, and Òrò, was accepted for presentation at the African Studies Association's 69th Annual Meeting in New Orleans this December. Both inform the Society's emerging technical and ethical approach to archival governance. Neither is an institutional accomplishment of the Society itself, and that distinction is one the Society insists on rather than blurs. Infrastructure is what the Society builds. Scholarship is what informs how it builds.

The mini-conference and panel discussion originally planned for this July have been postponed to 2027. This was sequencing, not retreat. Year Two's effort went toward the work described above and toward the scholarly record it produced; convening a public gathering before that foundation was finished would have asked the Society to perform a completion it had not yet earned. Foundation Day 2026 is observed as it was always meant to be: privately, and as a record rather than an event.

Year Three, running from July 14, 2026, to July 13, 2027, will consolidate what Year Two built rather than expand in every direction at once. Priorities include a public convening in 2027, continued growth of Per Medjat and its underlying catalog, clearer contribution and access-request workflows, further development of the visual ethnography archive, and governance documentation commensurate with an institution now old enough to need it. None of this is promised on a timeline that depends on funding or approval not yet secured. It is intention, stated as plainly as Year Two's intentions were stated a year ago.

The Society will grow only at the pace required to preserve trust, community authority, and scholarly integrity. That is not a limit on ambition. It is the ambition.

Délé Fágbèmí Ọ̀.
Founder and Steward
Iroko Historical Society
