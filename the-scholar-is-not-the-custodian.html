The Scholar Is Not the Custodian

On the difference between proximity and accountability
Iroko Commentary · Archives & Stewardship · May 2026
By Délé Fágbèmí Ọ̀.

A practitioner-archivist on scholarly access, initiatory obligation, and why communities sealed their archives long before archival theory had a word for what they were doing. The archive remembers who came in. It does not record what came due on the way out.

I hold initiatory titles in several Afro-Atlantic sacred traditions. I hold a JD, an MBA, and a graduate degree in history. I am completing a master's in library and information science. These credentials do not agree with each other about what I am permitted to disclose, to whom, and on what terms. The legal degree says: follow the law, respect confidentiality, consider harm. The scholarly training says: publish, document, describe. The initiatory obligation says: some things are not yours to name, regardless of your credentials.

Most scholars working with sacred and marginalized communities have never been forced to hold all three of those positions simultaneously. They have proximity without the obligation that should accompany it. That asymmetry is the problem I keep returning to.

Wade Davis is the most cited cautionary case. His 1985 book on Haitian Vodou and the alleged zombie phenomenon named practitioners, named substances, named societies, and provided enough structural information to become a tool of sensationalism and misrepresentation that Haitian religious communities are still managing four decades later. The harm was not malicious in any simple sense. Davis was working within the conventions of popular science writing as he understood them. He had the access, and he used it. The communities he documented had no mechanism to review, contest, or restrict what he published before it landed.

That is one version of the problem: the scholar who doesn't understand what access costs the people who granted it.

A recent news story reminded me that there is a second version. A prominent conservative policy architect holds a master's thesis on West African family structures among enslaved people in Virginia and a doctoral dissertation on the cultural construction of Louisiana's slave community in the early nineteenth century. His academic credentials were earned through sustained scholarly engagement with the history and culture of the communities his current institutional work targets. He is not a naive popularizer who stumbled into harm. He is a trained historian who studied the people his political project works against.

What interests me, as an archivist and as a practitioner, is the structural fact: scholarly access to a community's history does not generate an obligation to that community. Academic credentials confer no custodial responsibility. The archive opens to researchers. It has no mechanism for asking what they plan to do next.

The communities I work with in my scholarly and spiritual practice have understood this for a long time. Some of the most sophisticated archival traditions I have encountered in the African and Afro-Atlantic world are not in libraries. They are in societies that restrict access by grade, protect knowledge by oath, and maintain records through initiatory transmission rather than institutional deposit. These are not failures of preservation. They are governance decisions made by communities who have accurate assessments of what external legibility costs them.

Archival theory has a term for the absence these sealed archives produce: archival silence. But silence implies something is missing. In many of these cases, nothing is missing. The knowledge circulates inside the institution. The archive exists; it is simply not available to the scholar who arrives with institutional credentials and expects transparency as a professional courtesy.

Existing vocabulary stops there. But the condition is more specific than silence allows. A record or practice exists and is not activated as evidence because the conditions for its external legibility have not obtained, and may never be permitted to obtain. It is not silence. It is a decision, applied by the community to its own archive, on its own timeline, for reasons the community alone gets to evaluate.

The communities that have practiced this for generations were not being paranoid. They were being precise about risk.

Different intentions. The same architecture.

Scholarly access preceded any reckoning with obligation. In both cases, the communities documented had no meaningful input into how that documentation would be used. In both, the harm flowed from the distance between proximity and accountability.

I write this as someone who holds initiatory titles that come with specific obligations about what I can name, what I can describe, and what I am required to protect, regardless of whether publication would advance my career.

That obligation is not unusual. It is, in fact, the baseline ethical condition that every scholar working with sacred and marginalized communities should be required to reckon with before the first interview, the first archival visit, the first field note. The question is not only what the archive contains. It is what you owe the living people whose history you have been permitted to enter.

The archive remembers who came in. The communities whose records fill those archives are watching what they owed on the way out.
