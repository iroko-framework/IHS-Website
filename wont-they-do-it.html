Sacred Economy & Practice

Won’t They Do It

Righteousness, Debt, and the Fallacy of Spiritual ROI
Iroko Commentary · Sacred Economy & Practice · June 13, 2026
By Délé Fágbèmí Ọ̀.

On money rituals, testimony, sacred debt, and the danger of treating spiritual life as return on investment.

This essay examines how public testimony about answered spiritual work can turn gratitude into a ledger. It argues that material help is real and often necessary, but that spiritual worth cannot be measured by visible return. Against the logic of spiritual ROI, it proposes sacred obligation, righteous circulation, and discernment under hunger as better measures of religious life.

Won’t He do it!

The phrase travels through the religious life of Black communities as naturally in the pews of a Southern Baptist church as in the comments beneath a social media post about offerings, candles, and what the ancestors came through to deliver. In the church, the phrase often arrives as He. In this essay, I widen it to they, because the question moves beyond one Christian register into a broader world of spirits, ancestors, saints, orishas, lwa, the dead, and sacred forces that people call upon when life becomes materially dangerous. Sometimes it is a question. More often it is a pure proclamation, the verbal equivalent of a raised hand: won’t they do it. The road opened. The money came. The case turned. The lover returned. The child recovered. The threat passed over the house. The spirits showed up and showed out, and the evidence is here for anyone paying attention.

There is nothing shallow in that proclamation. People in material crisis do not need a spirituality that floats above the ground. They need help in the world where bodies hunger, landlords knock, children need shoes, bills accumulate, sickness eats savings, and the state has paperwork for every mercy it denies. The act of spiritual petition for material provision is ancient, universal, and honest. People have prayed for rain, harvests, safe childbirth, protection from enemies, luck in trade, and mercy in court across every culture that has left a record of its sacred life. Money rituals are not a modern corruption of religion. They are one contemporary expression of an ancient and legitimate human need. The poor do not ask spirits for money because they are greedy. They ask because poverty is violent.

And yet something shifts when gratitude becomes proof, proof becomes advertisement and advertisement becomes theology. What begins as testimony becomes a ledger. I gave something to the spirits. The spirits gave something back. I invested in offerings, study, travel, initiation, sacrifice, discipline, and attention. In return I received money, clients, protection, or some other legible good. Therefore, the spirits are real, the path is valid, the priest is powerful, the money was well spent, and I am favored.

This is the fallacy of spiritual ROI. ROI is a finance term that means return on investment, borrowed here intentionally. Financial terms carry their old habits with them: ledger, yield, cost, proof, debt, and collection. The point is to make visible the ledger language that contemporary spiritual culture has quietly imported, to hold it up to the light. To speak of spiritual ROI is to name the way many modern spiritual economies already invite us to see: ritual as investment, offering as capital, sacrifice as cost, testimony as proof, and blessing as return on a debt the sacred owes. In plainer terms, the danger is that we begin treating the sacred as if it must pay us back.

The fallacy is not that the spirits help materially. Across the Afro-Atlantic world and the broader theological world formed outside the metropole, by which I mean traditions shaped outside European bourgeois, secular, and protestant-capitalist assumptions, the sacred has always been understood to engage with practical human life. The fallacy is treating material return as the primary proof of spiritual worth, and in doing so treating the sacred as something that owes you. It is the mistake of confusing what came to you with what became of you.

መዝገብ ፩  |  Two Poles, One Ledger

The ROI logic operates at both extremes of contemporary spiritual life, and both deserve examination before either is dismissed.

At one pole sits the aesthetics of spiritual poverty. The image has significant cultural reach: the monk who has relinquished all attachment to worldly goods, the pilgrim who travels to a remote monastery giving up career and comfort, the ascetic who demonstrates spiritual seriousness through voluntary destitution. Sincerity is equated with sacrifice. Depth is equated with detachment. The life that is most aligned around spiritual development is expected to look, from the outside, like a steady giving up: less property, less comfort, less ambition, less attachment.

This tradition carries genuine insight. Attachment can be a spiritual obstacle. Accumulation for its own sake corrupts something essential. Some callings require material simplicity to be honored at all. True renunciation is not poverty as image. It is disciplined freedom from possession. It may be holy where it is chosen, accountable, and fruitful. The problem begins when renunciation becomes a credential, or when imposed poverty is treated as if its intent is spiritual formation. Yet the aesthetics of poverty as spiritual credential carries costs that are not evenly distributed.

Consider what a religious pilgrimage actually requires: the airfare, the accommodations, the months away from income-generating work, the study fees, the relationships strained by extended absence. These are not class-neutral expenditures. The person who can afford six months in Bahia, Havana, Benin, or Cap-Haïtien studying under a recognized teacher is not the same person working two jobs to keep the lights on in New Orleans or Hialeah. The spiritualization of poverty, the elevation of renunciation into a mark of serious practice, is most available to people with enough material security to make renunciation a choice rather than a condition. That spiritualization has a long history of counseling patience to the materially dispossessed, asking people whose poverty is structural and enforced to understand their suffering as spiritually instructive.

The Afro-Atlantic traditions offer a necessary correction, and their specificity matters here. Kumina, Haitian Vodou, Candomblé, Lucúmí, Hoodoo and the other rootwork traditions of the American South, the Congo-derived traditions that move through much of the Atlantic world were shaped under dispossession, carried through the Middle Passage, and kept alive under slavery, colonialism, racial exclusion, and systematic material danger. The people who preserved them could not romanticize poverty. They needed the sacred to engage with food, work, health, protection, luck, and trade, and they built traditions accordingly. These traditions generally do not make renunciation for its own sake the highest sign of spiritual seriousness. Wealth that arrives cleanly and moves through obligation is honored, not suspect. The question has never been whether abundance is acceptable. The question has always been what is the purpose of that abundance.

At the other pole sits spiritual capitalism. Here the divine operates as a service provider, offerings function as payments, and practice is understood as investment generating supernatural return. The prosperity gospel in its various Protestant forms is the most institutionally prominent version, but the logic moves through traditions well beyond Protestant Christianity. When the social media testimony is structured as an ROI report, when “Won’t they do it?” no longer means “I am grateful” but “my spiritual standing has been proven,” when the implicit argument is that they did it for me because my faith is sufficient, my standing recognized, my practice more productive than yours, the transactional frame has fully displaced the relational one. The communal response can then become part of the proof. The amens, shares, and affirmations do not merely receive the testimony; in a spiritual marketplace, they can help convert the testimony into authority.

The appeal of this framework is real and should not be dismissed without acknowledgment. There is something correct in the intuition that spiritual alignment and material flourishing are not opposed. The issue is the nature of the relationship this transactional model proposes, which is a contract: terms specified, accountability assigned for non-delivery, dissolved when terms are met. A contract does not require love, or depth, or the kind of orientation that persists when the terms are not honored. When the blessing does not arrive, the framework generates infinite explanation, insufficient offering, unresolved obstruction, procedural error, never asking whether return was the right measure to begin with or if a return was to be expected at all.

The question of worth (value) arises from both poles. The person who has spent years in serious practice, who has paid for ceremonies, initiations, and obligations, who has shaped major life decisions around spiritual commitments, is entitled to ask whether the arrangement is sustainable. The person who has spent money they could not easily afford on offerings and services is entitled to ask whether the return has justified the cost. These are not crass questions. They are honest ones. The problem is that the ROI frame cannot answer them adequately, because what is actually being asked is whether the life organized around these commitments has produced the right kind of person, and that question does not resolve on a schedule of debits and credits.

Both poles are operating inside the same ROI logic. The person who renounces worldly goods can fall into an inverse ledger, where less equals more: less money, less comfort, less property, and less attachment are treated as proof of greater spiritual seriousness. The prosperity devotee can fall into the direct ledger, where more equals more: more money, more clients, more visible blessing, and more material return are treated as proof that faith has been confirmed. Both measure sacred relationship by material output. Both proceed from the same foundational error: the divine does not owe you anything, and there is no guaranteed return.

መዝገብ ፪  |  The Direction of Debt

Across the Afro-Atlantic world and the broader non-metropole theological world, a convergent understanding of wealth and its movement has arisen through traditions shaped by comparable conditions: dispossession, colonial relationship, the necessity of building sacred life from survival rather than from comfort. It is not the claim of any single tradition and cannot be reduced to any single tradition’s vocabulary. But its emergence across traditions as different as the Vodou houses of Haiti, the terreiros of Brazil, the Ifá-centered practice of the Yorùbá-speaking world, the Congo-derived traditions of Cuba and Central Africa, the liberation theology communities of Latin America, and the African theological anthropologies developing in conversation with Ubuntu philosophy constitutes a cumulative argument no single tradition could make alone.

That argument centers on a claim about the direction of debt.

The ROI model casts the relationship between the petitioner and the sacred as a debt the sacred owes: I invested through offering and devotion, the spirits are therefore obligated to deliver return. When the return does not materialize, the model diagnoses a breach, something was done wrong, something was insufficient, and the account must be corrected before the debt can be collected. The sacred is cast as borrower, the devotee as lender collecting on a liability.

Across the traditions this essay draws from, this understanding is consistently inverted. The direction of sacred debt runs the other way. When the blessing arrives, it does not retire the devotee’s investment. It creates obligation on the devotee’s side, owed not to the sacred force that delivered it but outward, to the community, to the circulation, to the living and the dead and the not yet born who constitute the relational world through which sacred power moves. You are not the lender collecting on a debt. You are the channel through which something passes, temporarily, on its way to where it is going.

This is what righteous circulation names. In Vodou, the relationship between the living and the lwa is structured as ongoing service: reciprocal, continuous, demanding presence rather than periodic payment. In the terreiro economy of Candomblé, resources move through the house: the member who prospers feeds the community, sponsors the ceremony, sustains the ilé. In the Ubuntu-inflected African theological anthropologies, personhood is constituted through relationship, and wealth that does not circulate through relationship has been removed from the context that gives it meaning. In liberation theology’s basic community structures, what arrives through the community is held in trust for the community.

As the scholar J. Lorand Matory and others have argued, Afro-Atlantic religious worlds were not sealed survivals but moving, dialogic formations, shaped by travel, trade, return, memory, reinvention, and exchange. However they interacted historically, each gives us language for an older truth: abundance that does not circulate through obligation becomes spiritually suspect. A community that accumulated while its members went unfed was not spiritually advanced. It was dying. The theological intelligence that understands abundance as a resource for relational obligation is not a philosophical refinement. It is a survival technology that became, through centuries of practice and insistence, genuine theology.

Righteous circulation requires a prior condition. Resources cannot move through a relationship that is not rightly constituted. Before circulation comes righteous relationship: the ordered condition of one’s obligations to the dead, the living community, the sacred forces one is accountable to, and those not yet born who will inherit what is built and broken now.

Righteous relationship is not a state achieved and held. It is maintained through ongoing practice, through returning to the altar not because you have calculated a return but because the relationship requires presence. The dead require remembrance. The community requires sustenance. The sacred forces that move through your life require acknowledgment that is not transactional but relational. The ROI model damages righteous relationship at its root because it constitutes the relationship as a contract from the beginning. When you come to the altar as a creditor, when you approach the sacred as a borrower who owes you, you have not simply miscalculated the terms. You have established a wrong relationship where a righteous one needed to be.

A blessing, understood within righteous relationship and in service of righteous circulation, is not only a receipt. It is a summons. What the traditions ask of the person who has received is not display, not testimony as advertisement, not the performance of favor as evidence of rank. What the traditions ask is: what does this oblige you to do? Who must be fed with what arrived? What relationship must be honored that this blessing makes it possible to honor? What must circulate from this that does not stop at you?

The return on spiritual practice, measured in these terms, is not always legible as a financial return. It may be steadiness under pressure, the clarity of understanding what you are for, the community that holds you when the material situation does not resolve, the capacity to act from something other than panic. These do not appear on a balance sheet. The attempt to measure them on a balance sheet is precisely where the fallacy begins, because over time it teaches us to mistake spiritual fruit for measurable return.

መዝገብ ፫  |  What You Owe

None of this dissolves the hardest version of the question, asked not from philosophical curiosity but from genuine hunger: from the position of someone who has spent real money, real time, and real hope on practice that has produced no visible return, looking at a material situation that sacred vocabulary does not appear to be resolving.

The traditions do not guarantee material returns, and spiritual practice maintained with integrity can coexist with persistent material difficulty. This must be said plainly, without the smoothing that prosperity theology and spiritual marketing consistently apply. Many of the most revered practitioners across the Afro-Atlantic world have navigated material constraint without any indication that the sacred forces in their lives abandoned them. Poverty is not a sign of spiritual failure. Material difficulty is not evidence of blocked channels or insufficient practice.

What the traditions offer under material pressure is not guaranteed outcome but orientation. Desperation at its deepest level is not simply a condition of material lack. It is a condition of disorientation, not knowing what you are doing in the world or what your life is directed toward. The person who maintains righteous relationship and righteous circulation under pressure is not the same kind of desperate as the person who has neither. They may face comparable material constraints. They are not equally without ground.

Practical action is not the enemy of spiritual practice. The traditions are explicit that the sacred and practical must work together, that sacred practice does not narrow your options but requires you to deploy all of them, with your full intelligence and your full relationships. The spirits do not need your options to be narrow. When a practitioner escalates costs every time fear rises, when they make your crisis the proof of their power, they are offering something other than righteous relationship. Sacred authority does not need to terrorize the desperate to operate.

The reframe the traditions offer is not comfort for what has not resolved. It is a different question entirely. Won’t they do it asks whether the sacred delivered. The traditions ask something prior and more demanding: what did they do it for. Not as a question of divine motivation but as a question of your obligation. What is this for? What must now be done with it? What kind of person must you become because you were not abandoned? What must circulate from this that does not stop at you?

Don’t come to the altar collecting. The altar is not a bank. The ceremony is not a transaction. The relationship you hold with the sacred forces of your life, with your dead, with the traditions that carry you, is not a contract entered on the basis of projected returns. You come to honor, to remember, to maintain what requires maintenance, to be present to forces larger than your immediate need. If what you carry into the ceremony is the question of what you will get out of it, you have already answered it in a way that forecloses what righteous relationship could have given you, because a righteous relationship does not begin there.

This does not mean telling the hungry to be satisfied with meaning instead of bread. Bread still matters. Rent still matters. Medicine still matters. A tradition that answers hunger only with orientation has failed the body. But a tradition that answers hunger only with transaction may capture the soul. The task is to seek provision without surrendering discernment, to ask for help without letting hunger turn the altar into a marketplace or the practitioner into a creditor.

Won’t they do it? Yes, sometimes they do. But if the only question is whether they paid out, the relationship remains trapped in transaction. The question that opens something is what you owe now that they did.
